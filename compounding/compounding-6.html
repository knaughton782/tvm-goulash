So far, when referring to interest rates, we have used the stated, or nominal rate. Stated rates are usually expressed as an annual percentage, which can be a bit misleading when compounding is involved. The next example shows how nominal and actual interest rates can differ due to compounding.

Let's continue with our original example but assume semi-annual compounding.

The bank pays interest every 6 months. The first interest payment, after 6 months, is calculated based on the original investment PLUS the first interest payment. Every 6 months, the amound of interest paid increases. Visually, semi-annual compounding looks like this:

Since interest is compounded semi-annually, half of 10% (or 5%)would be credited to the account after 6 months and the other half at the end of the year.

Notice that the second interest payment is higher than the first. Interest earned during the first term will earn interest in the second term. By compounding, we earned 0.25% more than the nominal rate (i.e., what we would make with annual compounding). Therefore, actual interest is 10.25%.
