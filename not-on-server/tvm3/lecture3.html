Introduction

Cash Flows

Now we'll modify the equation even further, this time to introduce cash flows.

Cash flows are defined as:

Regularly scheduled, equal payments over the term of the investment or loan.

By adding cash flows, the TVM equation really starts to become useful in your everyday life. Cash flows are prevalent in personal and business finance. As a lender, you may receive cash flows in the form of dividends or coupon payments. As a borrower, you may be required to make payments on your car or mortgage.

TVM: Cash Flows

Perpetuities

Perpetuities

A perpetuity is a series of equal-sized, regularly-scheduled payments paid indefinitly.

To break that down:

  • equal-sized: every payment is the same amount
  • regularly-scheduled: payments are received at regular intevals (e.g., monthly, annually, or bi-weekly)
  • indefinite: payments will continue to be paid forever
Perpetuities are pretty rare. One example is a preferred stock that pays a guaranteed fixed dividend to the shareholder for the life of the company, which is assumed to be infinite. In finance, we are usually concerned with the Present Value (PV) of a perpetuity. For instance:
  • "How much should I pay for that preferred stock with guaranteed fixed dividends?"
  • "How much should I ask for today, if I promise to pay someone a certain amount regularly and indefinitly?"

Annuities

Annuities

An annuity is a regularly-scheduled, equal-sized cash flows over a specified, finite period of time.

To break that down:

  • equal-sized: every payment is the same amount
  • regularly-scheduled: payments are received at regular intevals (e.g., monthly, annually, or bi-weekly)
  • specified priod: when the term ends, payments stop
Annuities are similar to perpetuities except that annuities cease after a specific number of payments.
  • Mortgages
  • Car loans, Credit cards, and other loans
  • Automatic contributions from your paycheck to a retirement account (e.g., 401k)
  • A bond with coupon payments
At some point in your life, you will have one, if not all, of the annuities listed above. Therefore, you will find TVM with cash flows very useful in personal and professional decision making.

TVM: Cash Flows

Annuities

There are two types of annuities:

  • Regular Annuity, also known as an Ordinary Annuity
  • Annuity Due

The difference is in the timing of cash flows.

  • Regular annuity: cash flow arrives at the end of each period
  • Annuity due: cash flow arrives at the beginning of each period

If the problem does not specifiy whether you are dealing with a regular annuity or an annuity due, or if it doesn't state when payments are made, assume that it is a regular annuity.

  • Insurance Premiums are due up front

Most annuities are ordinary annuities.

FVA Factor

A fourth way to calculate FVA is using a table of FVA factors. Mathematically, the FVA Factor represents the calculation within the brackets of the FVA formula.

If you know the FVA Factor, you can simply multiply it by CF to find FVA. Let's solve the same problem again using an FVA Factors table.
