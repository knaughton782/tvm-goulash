TVM: The Basics

Introduction

Time Value of Money (TVM) is the equation we use to convert between today's money and money in the future. Transactions operate on this principal every day.

Businesses use the equation to compare investments alternatives, to assist decision-makers by converting project opportunities into dollars, or to borrow money from lenders. Individuals use the TVM equation to plan for retirement savings or to compare the cost of a prospective mortgage or auto loan.

One of the central concepts of finance is that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future. For example, if you make a purchase with a credit card, interest is added to the bill to compensate the credit card company for lending you money. Conversely, if you deposit money in a savings account, the bank pays you interest to compensate you for lending them money.

You usually collect customer accounts at the end of the month, but one customer requests an extension. If you forego the payment for a while, you should be compensated for the delay.

Most lenders would want to know:

  • How much is being borrowed?
  • How long will it take the customer to repay the loan?
  • What is the risk that the customer will not be able to pay the account at a later date?
  • What else could your company do with the money if the customer paid now?
  • If the loan is long enough, will inflation further erode the value of tomorrow's dollar?

To review:

  • We calculated the 1st year of interest earned by multiplying the interest rate, 10%, times the 1st year starting principal, $100.
    ($100 * 10%) = $10
  • We calculated the 2nd year of interest earned by finding 10% of the 2nd year starting principal, $110.

In other words, we calculated the interest earned in each individual year and added all the interest payments back to the principal to find the Future Value (FV).

TVM: The Basics

Equation

Although calculating the interest for each individual year and adding it to the principal does produce the correct answer, what would you do if asked for the future value after 100 years? You could create a spreadsheet of formulas with 100 interest payment calculations. However, its time consuming and fortunately, unncessary.

Instead, we'll derive a general equation that will allow you to solve for Future Value (FV), Present Value (PV), Interest rate (i), or number of years (n).

Let's look back at our previous calculations:

  1. Figure out the 1st year of interest by finding 10% of $100.
    (100 * 0.10) = 10
  2. Figure out the 2nd year of interest by finding 10% of $110.
    (110 * 0.10) = 11
  3. Add the 1st and 2nd year interest to the principal of 100.
    100 + 10 + 11 = $121
  4. Now look at these steps as one equation.
  5. Let's replace the numbers with variables to make it easier to manipulate the equation.

10

TVM: The Basics

Equation

Now that we have variables in place of numbers, its just a matter of simplifying the formula and grouping like variables.

[(PV + i)] + [(PV * i) + PV] * i + PV

  1. Start with the parenthesis by multiplying the variables within.
    PVi + [PVi + PV] * i + PV
  2. Distribute the multiplication to the variables within the middle bracket.
    PVi + PVi2 + PVi + PV
  3. Group like variables.
    PVi + PVi2 + PVi + PV  ⟶  PVi2 + 2PVi + PV
  4. Use the distributive property to separate the like term, PV.
    PV i2 + 2PV i + PV  ⟶   PV (i2 + 2i + 1)
11

TVM: The Basics

Equation

The equation in the parentheses can be simplified to (x + 1)(x + 1). You can find information on this equation in the math help under FOIL.

PV (i2 + 2i + 1)  ⟶   PV(i + 1)(i + 1)

Think of (i + 1) as a number. A number multiplied by itself is that number squared.

PV(i + 1)(i + 1)  ⟶   PV(i + 1)2

Remember back to the problem: the term is 2 years. Therefore, n = 2. We can replace the number in the exponent with the variable.

PV( i + 1) 2  ⟶   PV( i + 1) n

TVM: The Basics

Equation

This equation provides the future value so let's add FV to the other side of the equal sign.

FV = PV(1 + i)n

We could continue to derive the equation for 3, 10, 100 years. After a lot of algebraic manipulation, the outcome would be the same. Its not important that you're able to repeat the derivation, but that you understand the equation can be derived from individual calculations.

Now that we have a general equation, let's try some examples. Answer this question:

First, identify the variables from the problem:

Here are some tips for identifying the variables.

  • i is a rate. Look for a percentage or decimal.
  • n is an expression of time or number of years.

Equation

Once you've identified the variables, plug the numbers into the equation:

FV = PV(1 + i) n
PV = 100     FV = ?     i = 10% or 0.10     n = 10
  1. Starting with the parentheses, add the numbers in place of the variables and do the addition.
    FV = 100(1 + 0.10) 10  ⟶  FV = 100(1.10) 10

  2. Next, apply the exponent. Be careful about rounding.
    FV = 100(2.5937424601)

  3. Finally, do the multiplication.
    FV = $259.37

TVM: The Basics

Equation

In the problem we just solved, the number in parentheses is called the Future Value Factor. In the ‘good old days’ before calculators, people used tables of pre-calculated Future Value Factors to solve the TVM equation.

First, find the intersection of the number of periods (years) on the left and the interest rate at the top to find the Future Value Factor. Then multiply PV times the Future Value Factor to calculate FV.


FV = PV(Future Value Factor)

FV = (100 * 2.5937)

FV = $259.37

Then solve for the unknown variable, in this case n.

  1. P: Add the numbers in the parentheses.
    1500 = 1000(1.09)n
  2. MD: Isolate the parentheses by dividing each side by 1000. Then convert the fraction on the left side of the equal sign to a decimal.
  3. E: Many people will get stuck here. To manipulate an unknown exponent, take the natural log (ln) of each side. ln is a button on your calculator. Pay attention to what happens to the exponent n.
    ln 1.5 = n(ln 1.09)
  4. MD: To isolate n, divide both sides by (ln 1.09). Then solve for n.
