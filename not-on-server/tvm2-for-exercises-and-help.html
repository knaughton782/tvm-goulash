TVM: Compound Interest

Compound vs. Simple Interest

Compound Interest

To state it simply, compound Interest is when interest earns interest.

Compounding takes place on some sort of regular schedule. When solving word problems, you'll look for key words that indicate how often interest is compounded:

  • annually (1 time per year)
  • semi-annually (2 times per year)
  • quarterly (4 times per year)
  • monthly (12 times per year)
  • daily (365 times per year or 366 times per year in a leap year)

If there is no specifically stated compounding schedule, you should assume interest is compounded annually.


Simple Interest

The alternative to compound interest is simple interest, which means that interest is only paid once at the end of the investment.

Simple interest = principal * interest rate * time

Example:
20 = 100 * 10% * 2

Nominal vs. Actual Interest Rates

So far, when referring to interest rates, we have used the stated, or nominal rate.

Nominal rate = Stated rate

Stated rates are usually expressed as an annual percentage, which can be a bit misleading when compounding is involved. The next example shows how nominal and actual interest rates can differ due to compounding.

Let's continue with our original example but assume semi-annual compounding.

The bank pays interest every 6 months. The first interest payment, after 6 months, is calculated based on the original investment PLUS the first interest payment. Every 6 months, the amound of interest paid increases. Visually, semi-annual compounding looks like this:

Since interest is compounded semi-annually, half of 10% (or 5%)would be credited to the account after 6 months and the other half at the end of the year.

Notice that the second interest payment is higher than the first. Interest earned during the first term will earn interest in the second term. By compounding, we earned 0.25% more than the nominal rate (i.e., what we would make with annual compounding). Therefore, actual interest is 10.25%.

TVM: Compound Interest

Effective Annual Rate

Now that we have calculated annual, semi-annual, and daily compounding while keeping all other variables the same, it should be clear that the nominal (stated rate) isn't a very accurate way to evaluate a financial option.

All of the problems we have calculated so far have been the same, except for the compounding period, with the following results:

  • FV with Annual Compounding: $110.00
  • FV with Semi-Annual Compounding: $110.25
  • FV with Daily Compounding: $110.52

Yet all have a nominal rate of interest of 10%.

If compounding occurs more than once a year, the stated annual interest rate must be adjusted, so that you can compare apples to apples. The adjusted interest rate is known as the Effective Annual Rate (EAR).

The EAR is the equivalent rate of an annually compounded security to that of a security with multiple compounding periods.

Truth in advertising rules have been established to help clear up consumer confusion. Banks must advertise the EAR (also called the annual percentage yield in some texts and advertisements).

Choosing the Right FD Based on your Calculations

If you've been able to calculate EAR, but aren't sure how to interpret the data, remember:

  • For an annually compounded Fixed Deposit (FD), the stated rate is the same as EAR. Therefore, the nominal interest rate for the FD from Bank B is already in terms of EAR.
  • EAR converts the quarterly compounded interest into annually compounded terms so you can compare the two FD rates.
  • Choose the FD with the higher EAR, because it will lead to a higher FV when all other conditions are equal.

The FD from Bank A has a lower rate of return than the FD at Bank B. Therefore, the Future Value of the FD at Bank B is higher than the FD at Bank A.

From the examples already shown, you can see that when two finance options havae different interest rates and compounding periods, you cannot evaluate them by comparing the stated interest rates.

  • Given two choices with different interest rates and compounding periods, the option with a higher interest rate does not always have a higher Future Value.
  • Given two choices with different interest rates and compounding periods, the option that compounds more frequently does not always have a higher Future Value.

The options' nominal interest rates have to be converted to EAR, so that you have similar rates to compare.
