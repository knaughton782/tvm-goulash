Now that we have calculated annual, semi-annual, and daily compounding while keeping all other variables the same, it should be clear that the nominal (stated rate) isn't a very accurate way to evaluate a financial option. All of the problems we have calculated so far have been the same, except for the compounding period, with the following results.

How much money would you have in 1 year if you invested $100 at a rate of 10%?

  • Future Value (FV) with Annual Compounding: $110.00
  • Future Value (FV) with Semi-Annual Compounding: $110.25
  • Future Value (FV) with Daily Compounding: $110.52

Yet all have a nominal rate of interest of 10%.

If compounding occurs more than once a year, the stated annual interest rate must be adjusted, so that you can compare apples to apples. The adjusted interest rate is known as the Effective Annual Rate (EAR). Truth in advertising rules have been established to help clear up consumer confusion. Banks must advertise the EAR (also called the annual percentage yield in some texts and advertisements).

The EAR is the equivalent rate of an annually compounded security to that of a security with multiple compounding periods.
